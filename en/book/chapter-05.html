5.1 — Clavicle Fracture Analysis with o3

On a Wednesday evening in November 2024, cycling home through the dark streets of Milan, an invisible pothole appeared. The bike stopped; I didn't. The impact was on my left shoulder, direct and violent. The pain was immediate and unmistakable: something was broken. At the emergency room, an X-ray and a diagnosis: compound fracture of the middle third of the left clavicle. Figure-eight brace, painkillers, orthopedic appointment in four days. End of standard procedure.

But I had a second doctor available. The next day, from my couch, I photographed the X-rays with my smartphone and uploaded them to ChatGPT, using the o3 model — OpenAI's latest, with advanced visual reasoning capabilities (🩹 ff.124 Ah-AI!). The response came in 90 seconds. Ninety seconds for an analysis that included: identification of fracture type (compound, middle third), evaluation of fragment alignment, pseudarthrosis risk assessment, therapeutic recommendation (conservative treatment with brace), expected healing timeline (6-8 weeks), and a progressive rehabilitation plan with passive mobilization exercises from week three.

Four days later, the orthopedist confirmed the diagnosis at 95%. The 5% difference? The orthopedist noticed a slight angulation that might require monitoring — a detail that o3 had mentioned as "possibility to verify with lateral projection" but hadn't emphasized. In essence: the model was right about the diagnosis, right about the therapy, right about the prognosis, and had even intuited the limit of its own analysis by suggesting further investigation.

“The future of medicine is not AI replacing the doctor. It's the doctor without AI being replaced by the doctor with AI.”
— Eric Topol, Deep Medicine

What does this mean? Not that ChatGPT replaces the orthopedist. It means that the distance between a professional consultation and an automated analysis has shrunk from a chasm to a crack, and that crack narrows with every model update. Consider the numbers: an orthopedic consultation in Italy costs between 100 and 200 euros in the private sector, with an average latency of 3-7 days. The o3 analysis cost zero euros (included in the $20/month ChatGPT Plus subscription) with a latency of 90 seconds. The cost-time ratio is three orders of magnitude.

But the numbers don't capture the most important aspect: reduced anxiety. Between the fall and the orthopedic consultation, I would have spent four days in uncertainty — is it serious? Do I need surgery? How long will it last? The o3 analysis answered all these questions the day after the fall. Not with absolute certainty (the model is explicit about its limits: "this analysis does not replace professional advice"), but with enough detail to transform anxiety into an action plan.

The experiment also revealed the current limits of medical AI. I tried uploading the same images to Anthropic's Claude: it refused to analyze them, citing ethical policies on medical image analysis. Google's Gemini provided a more generic analysis, correct but less detailed than o3. The difference between models isn't just technical: it's philosophical. OpenAI chose to allow medical analysis with disclaimers; Anthropic chose caution. Both positions are defensible; neither is definitive.

The deepest implication is systemic. Globally, 3.6 billion people lack access to basic healthcare. An AI model running on a $100 smartphone that analyzes an X-ray in 90 seconds isn't a gadget: it's portable healthcare infrastructure. Arianna Huffington founded Thrive AI Health on this premise: AI as a "family doctor" accessible 24/7, in every language (🩹 ff.109 Doctor in the famil-AI). The WHO estimates a global shortage of 10 million healthcare workers by 2030. AI won't close the gap, but it can serve as intelligent triage — routing urgent cases to human doctors and autonomously handling low-risk diagnoses.

My clavicle, six weeks later, healed perfectly. The figure-eight brace ended up in a drawer. The orthopedist, at the follow-up visit, commented: "Normal healing." Exactly what o3 had predicted, the day after the fall, from my couch, for zero additional euros. The fracture healed; the question remains open: in a world where a language model reads an X-ray better than most general practitioners, what does "healing" mean?

The answer, perhaps, is that healing has never been just about diagnosis. It's about presence, listening, clinical judgment integrated with the patient's history. AI excels at the first; the doctor remains irreplaceable for the second. The best future is neither purely human nor purely artificial: it's a centaur — half intuition, half algorithm — that heals better than either alone.

5.2 — AI and Longevity: Compressing 150 Years into a Month

Longevity research has always suffered from a paradoxical temporal problem: studying aging takes decades, but those funding the research want results in years. David Sinclair, Harvard geneticist and pioneer of anti-aging research, summarized the challenge with a number: 150 years. That's how long it would have taken, with traditional methods, to systematically test the combinations of molecules that might slow cellular aging. Then generative artificial intelligence arrived, and those 150 years compressed into a month (📎 Generative AI accelerates longevity research). Not only that: the cost dropped by a billion-fold.

The numbers sound like hyperbole, but they reflect a structural change in how we do science. AI models don't replace researcher intuition; they accelerate it. They can simulate thousands of molecular interactions in silico before a single experiment is conducted in the lab. They can identify patterns in genomic datasets that a human would take years to notice. They can suggest compound combinations that no one would have ever tested — not because they were unreasonable, but because the possibility space was simply too vast to explore manually.

“Living organisms live for approximately 100 million respiratory cycles — a rule that unites creatures from bacteria to whales.”
— Geoffrey West, physicist and complexity theorist

But longevity isn't just basic research. It's also early diagnosis. Researchers have demonstrated that cancer-specific DNA fragments can be detected in blood more than three years before clinical diagnosis (📎 Circulating DNA and early cancer diagnosis). Three years. In oncology, three years of lead time can transform a sentence into a cure. AI makes this detection scalable: a model trained on millions of samples can identify signals that would escape any panel of human experts.

And then there's the most ambitious frontier: reprogramming the immune system to fight cancer permanently. Scientists at UCLA have reprogrammed a patient's stem cells to continuously produce T cells that attack tumors (📎 Reprogrammed stem cells fight cancer). Not a therapy to be repeated periodically, but a permanent modification to the body's source code. The question is no longer "can we cure cancer?" but "can we transform it into a manageable chronic condition, like diabetes?"

The emerging pattern is clear: AI is shifting medicine from reaction to prevention, from cure to maintenance, from the doctor who intervenes when something breaks to the system that continuously monitors and intervenes before damage becomes irreversible. A recent study found that 4,000 steps per day reduce mortality risk by 9-39% (📎 4,000 daily steps and longevity). Simple information, but it only becomes actionable when a system (app, wearable, AI assistant) transforms it into personalized reminders based on individual behavior.

There's an irony in all this. Longevity research has long been considered a luxury for billionaires obsessed with immortality — the various Bryan Johnsons with their young plasma transfusions. But AI democratizes this field too. A model that costs cents per query can provide nutritional recommendations, analyze sleep patterns, suggest screenings based on risk factors. You won't live forever, but it might help you live better for the decades you have.

Estrogen replacement therapy, for example, is associated with slower aging of the brain, immune system, liver, and arteries (📎 Estrogens and organ aging). A temporal analysis of tissue aging reveals an inflection around age 50, with blood vessels aging prematurely (📎 When tissues start aging). This data existed, but it was fragmented, buried in academic papers, inaccessible to the average patient. AI turns it into a conversation.

Meta's TRIBE model is the first to predict brain responses to stimuli with one billion parameters (📎 TRIBE: predicting brain responses). Gamma rhythm stimulation can treat neurological disorders, being critical for memory and learning (📎 Gamma stimulation and neurology). Each discovery is a piece; AI is the mosaicist assembling them into a coherent picture.

My clavicle healed in six weeks. My brain, my heart, my arteries will continue aging for decades. But perhaps — perhaps — the way they age can change. Not because someone discovered the elixir of youth, but because someone built a system that connects millions of partial discoveries into a navigable map. Longevity isn't a destination; it's a process. And that process, for the first time in history, is accelerated by machines that learn faster than we age.
